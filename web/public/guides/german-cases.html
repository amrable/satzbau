The four German cases explained

Nominative, accusative, dative, genitive — what each one does and how to spot it.

German marks the role of a noun in a sentence using case. Instead of relying on word order (as English does), German changes the articles and some endings. There are four cases: nominative, accusative, dative, and genitive. Learning them is the single biggest investment that pays off in every German sentence you read or write.

Nominative — the subject

The nominative is the "who is doing the action" case. It's the form you find in a dictionary.

  • Der Hund schläft. — the dog is the subject.
  • Also used after sein, werden, bleiben: Das ist ein Student.

Accusative — the direct object

The accusative marks the thing being directly acted on.

  • Ich sehe den Mann. — den Mann is what I see.
  • Always after these prepositions: durch, für, gegen, ohne, um.
  • After two-way prepositions when there's motion toward a destination.

Dative — the indirect object

The dative marks the recipient — "to whom" or "for whom" something happens.

  • Ich gebe dem Kind einen Apfel. — dem Kind is the recipient.
  • Always after these prepositions: aus, bei, mit, nach, seit, von, zu, gegenüber.
  • After two-way prepositions when describing static location.
  • With dative-only verbs: helfen, danken, folgen, gefallen, gehören, antworten, etc.
  • Plural nouns add an -n in dative if they don't already end in -n or -s: mit den Kindern.

Genitive — possession and formal prepositions

Genitive expresses possession or relationship. It's slightly formal; spoken German often replaces it with von + dative.

  • Das Auto meines Vaters. — my father's car.
  • Masculine and neuter singular nouns add -s or -es: des Mannes, des Hauses.
  • Genitive prepositions: (an)statt, trotz, während, wegen, außerhalb, innerhalb.
  • In conversation, wegen dem Regen is common (dative); in writing, wegen des Regens is standard.

How to identify a case in a sentence

  1. Find the verb. Is it a dative-only verb? If yes, its object is dative.
  2. Find a preposition. If it's in a fixed accusative or dative list, the case is decided.
  3. Otherwise, ask wer?/was? → nominative. wen?/was? → accusative. wem? → dative.
