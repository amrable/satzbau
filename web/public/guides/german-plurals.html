German plural rules

Five endings, five patterns. Plus how to predict which noun uses which.

German has five main plural endings: -n/-en, -e, -er, -s, and no ending at all. Some also add an umlaut (ä, ö, ü) on the stem vowel. There's no single rule, but noun gender and ending make most plurals predictable. In the plural, every article is die, regardless of the singular gender.

The five endings at a glance

EndingTypical nounsExamples
-n / -enmost feminine nounsdie Frau → Frauen, die Blume → Blumen, die Zeitung → Zeitungen
-e (± umlaut)many masculine and some neuterder Tag → Tage, der Stuhl → Stühle, das Jahr → Jahre
-er (+ umlaut if possible)many one-syllable neuter nounsdas Kind → Kinder, das Haus → Häuser, das Buch → Bücher
-sforeign/loan words, words ending in a voweldas Auto → Autos, das Hotel → Hotels, der Park → Parks
no ending (± umlaut)masc/neut ending in -er, -en, -el, -chen, -leinder Lehrer → Lehrer, der Vater → Väter, das Mädchen → Mädchen

Patterns by gender

Feminine nouns

~90% take -(e)n. Almost automatic.

  • die Lampe → Lampen, die Tasche → Taschen, die Universität → Universitäten
  • Exceptions with umlaut + -e: die Hand → Hände, die Stadt → Städte, die Maus → Mäuse.

Masculine nouns

Most take -e, often with umlaut.

  • der Hund → Hunde, der Zug → Züge, der Arzt → Ärzte
  • Those ending in -er, -en, -el usually take no ending (maybe umlaut): der Bruder → Brüder, der Wagen → Wagen, der Apfel → Äpfel.
  • "Weak" masculines add -(e)n: der Junge → Jungen, der Student → Studenten.

Neuter nouns

No single rule, but:

  • One-syllable neuter nouns often take -er with umlaut: das Kind → Kinder, das Buch → Bücher.
  • Neuter nouns ending in -chen or -lein never change: das Mädchen → Mädchen.
  • Loan words take -s: das Auto → Autos.

Dative plural rule

In the dative plural, the noun adds an -n if it doesn't already end in -n or -s.

  • die Kinder → mit den Kindern
  • die Bücher → in den Büchern
  • die Autos → mit den Autos (no change, ends in -s)

Learn the plural with the noun

The best habit is to learn every new noun with its article and its plural: die Hand, die Hände. Writing vocabulary as three items (article, noun, plural) means you never have to reconstruct the plural from guesses.
