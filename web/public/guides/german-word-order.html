German word order (Satzbau)

The three rules that cover 95% of German sentences.

German word order feels unpredictable at first, but it's governed by a small number of rules. The most important one is that the conjugated verb must be the second element in a main clause — almost always. Once you internalize this, German sentences start clicking into place.

Rule 1: V2 — the verb is always second in a main clause

The conjugated verb occupies the second "position" (not necessarily the second word — the second unit).

  • Ich gehe heute ins Kino. (Ich / gehe / heute / ins Kino)
  • Heute gehe ich ins Kino. (Heute / gehe / ich / ins Kino)
  • Ins Kino gehe ich heute. (Ins Kino / gehe / ich / heute)

The fronted element changes emphasis but the verb stays in position 2. You can move anything to the front, but the verb won't budge.

Rule 2: subordinate clauses send the verb to the end

After subordinating conjunctions like weil, dass, wenn, obwohl, als, während, damit, ob, the conjugated verb goes to the very end.

  • Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich krank bin.
  • Ich weiß, dass du Recht hast.
  • Wenn es regnet, nehme ich einen Regenschirm. (wenn-clause first → main clause starts with the verb to keep V2 globally)

Rule 3: in Perfekt/modal/future, the non-finite verb goes to the end

When the sentence has more than one verb (auxiliary + participle, or modal + infinitive), the conjugated verb is in position 2 and the other verb goes to the end.

  • Ich habe gestern ein Buch gelesen.
  • Er will morgen nach Berlin fahren.
  • Wir werden nächste Woche unseren Freund besuchen.

This is called the Satzklammer (sentence bracket): the verbs form a frame around the rest of the clause.

TeKaMoLo — the order of adverbial info

When a sentence has several adverbials (time, cause, manner, place), German tends to put them in this order:

  • Temporal (when?)
  • Kausal (why?)
  • Modal (how?)
  • Lokal (where?)

Example: Ich fahre morgen (Te) wegen der Arbeit (Ka) mit dem Zug (Mo) nach München (Lo).

You can front one of these elements to emphasize it (then the verb still comes next, per V2), but their internal order stays TeKaMoLo.

Object order: dative before accusative (usually)

When both objects are nouns, dative comes first: Ich gebe dem Kind einen Apfel.

When one is a pronoun, the pronoun comes first: Ich gebe ihn (acc) dem Kind (dat).

Yes/no questions and commands

In yes/no questions and imperatives, the verb is in position 1:

  • Kommst du mit?
  • Geh nach Hause!
